# My Second Country: revenue and traffic operating plan (the six lanes)

Date: 2026-06-27. Status: design, pending review. Companion graphic: the one-page swimlane roadmap.

## 1. Goal and the spine

Turn high-intent, low-volume relocation traffic into revenue, without breaking the fence.

The spine, in one line: the traffic lanes (5 and 6) bring high-intent people in, the money lanes
(1 to 4) convert them, you build the minimum, and traffic plus trust unlock the rest.

The framing that decides what to build (Jobs to Be Done, Christensen): nobody hires this site to
"read relocation content." They hire it at one moment, when the rules just changed, they cannot
tell what is current, they are about to make an irreversible expensive move, and they are afraid of
getting it wrong. The real competitor is the stale forum thread and the first lawyer they Google.
The money is not in the appeal of the data; it is in resolving the anxiety and the inertia at that
moment. So we monetise the moment of maximum fear, priced in the unit of the decision, not the
pageview. That is why display ads and a premium subscription are the wrong models here and a
pre-screened referral is the right one.

## 2. The fence (the constraint that governs every lane)

The site gives screening intelligence, never advice, and routes to licensed professionals. Revenue
must reinforce that, not erode it.

- Every monetised surface routes to a professional or a service; it never gives a personal verdict
  or tells a specific reader what to do. "Confirm with a licensed professional" is the product, and
  the referral is that same action.
- Paid relationships are disclosed (ASA in the UK, FTC for US readers): a plain line that an
  introduction or link may earn a fee.
- The cited-credibility moat is the asset. No pay-to-rank listicles, no selling placement to the
  highest bidder, no unvetted firms. A single bad referral does more damage than a month of revenue.
- Highest legal care sits on lane 1 (introductions) and lane 3 (a personalised brief). Each has a
  fence-safe framing spelled out in its section below.

## 3. Phasing and the gates between phases

- **Phase 0, foundations (this week):** email capture, pick the first corridor, finish the GSC key.
- **Phase 1, turn it on (now, continuous):** lanes 5 and 6 run from day one; lane 1 runs as a
  concierge test on the one corridor.
- **Phase 2, first money (gate: one professional has actually paid for an introduction, and a
  corridor is pulling measurable traffic):** lane 2; widen lane 1 to more corridors.
- **Phase 3, scale (gate: at least one corridor has proven, repeatable traffic):** lane 3; lane 4.

The gates are deliberate. Do not start Phase 2 on hope; start it when a pro has paid once. Do not
start Phase 3 until a corridor's traffic is repeatable, because lanes 3 and 4 only pay at volume.

## 4. Foundations (Phase 0)

**Email capture.** The one asset that compounds, and the fix for the leaky bucket (today every
visitor arrives once and is gone). Mechanism that fits the fence and feeds lane 2: a "get told when
these rules change" capture on the high-intent pages (regime, dealbreaker, golden-visa pages), since
the reader's genuine fear is exactly that the rules will move under them. This doubles as the
audience for a future reader newsletter and as demand evidence for lane 2. Keep the tooling boring:
a hosted form into one list (the existing stack already loads no heavy client code; do not add a
framework for this). Success: the list exists and grows with traffic; no growth means the traffic or
the offer is wrong, which is itself a useful signal.

**Pick the first corridor.** Criteria: depth of cited data we already hold, a nameable genuinely
good professional we can stand behind, and early demand signal. Candidates, strongest first:
Portugal IFICI / NHR closure (huge stale-search volume, a clear "am I still eligible" fear, advisers
plentiful) and the Greece golden-visa tiers (high affiliate value, active buyers). Recommendation:
**Portugal IFICI / NHR** as corridor one. Confirm before Phase 1.

**Finish the GSC key (optional, when patient).** It tells us which corridors actually pull traffic,
which is the evidence that later picks the lane-4 corridor and confirms the lane-1 corridor.

## 5. The six lanes in detail

Each lane: what it is, the mechanism, who pays or what it does, build vs ops, first steps, the
success metric and the kill criteria, and the risk or fence note.

### Lane 1: the hand-off (money; build + ops; START HERE)

- **What:** at the exact point a page tells a reader they are disqualified or qualified, offer one
  vetted professional for that specific corridor, with the reader's own screener inputs attached as
  a structured brief. The professional pays per accepted introduction, not per click.
- **Mechanism:** a "request a vetted introduction" block on the corridor's dealbreaker and qa pages.
  The reader's situation (the inputs they already gave the screener) travels with the intro, so the
  lead is pre-qualified and arrives at peak intent. Pricing is per accepted intro, in the unit of the
  decision.
- **Build vs ops:** the block is the smallest real build; everything else is ops (securing the pro,
  vetting, brokering, invoicing). The first version is concierge: the block emails you and the
  reader, and you broker the first introductions by hand. No platform, no automation, until it is
  proven a pro will pay.
- **First steps:** (1) secure one professional for the chosen corridor (see the vetting process,
  section 6); (2) build the intro block on that corridor's pages; (3) broker the first five intros by
  hand and charge a flat fee.
- **Success metric:** at least one professional pays for at least one accepted introduction.
  **Kill criteria:** after a reasonable number of qualified intros, no pro will pay, or readers will
  not request intros; then the moment is not as monetisable as believed and we lean harder on lanes 2
  and 5/6.
- **Risk and fence:** a bad pro destroys the moat, so vetting gates expansion. Framed as "we do not
  advise; here is a professional we have vetted for your situation," with disclosure. Per-intro
  pricing needs the pro to trust lead quality, which is unproven until there is a track record, hence
  the concierge start.
- **Who pays:** boutique cross-border CPAs and immigration / residency-by-investment firms who today
  buy low-quality leads from comparison sites.

### Lane 2: the early-warning bell (money; ops, reuse; Phase 2)

- **What:** sell the pros a paid alert when a rule in their corridor changes.
- **Mechanism:** the freshness and staleness engine plus the weekly digest already exist. Scope a
  per-corridor watch (Greece 5B/5A, Portugal IFICI, Spain Beckham, Cyprus and Malta non-dom) that
  pings a subscribing practitioner the day a threshold, law, or deadline moves, with the cited
  source. "Your clients will ask about this tomorrow; here it is today, sourced." Recurring, per
  practitioner.
- **Build vs ops:** near-zero new build (repackage the digest); the work is sales to the same pros
  lane 1 recruits.
- **First steps:** after lane 1 has a pro paying, scope the alert and pitch it to three advisers you
  would want as referral partners anyway.
- **Success metric:** one recurring paying practitioner. **Kill criteria:** pros say their existing
  sources are good enough and will not pay; then it stays a free trust-builder for lane-1 recruiting.
- **Risk:** small market (boutiques), so willingness to pay must be real and recurring; the value is
  the cited rigor and the breadth across corridors versus their status quo.

### Lane 3: the paid cheat-sheet (money; build; Phase 3)

- **What:** a one-time paid artifact, not a subscription. The reader enters their corridor and facts
  and gets a dated, cited brief of the rules that apply to them, the dealbreakers, and the exact
  questions to ask, built to hand to their lawyer so they do not pay professional rates for the
  basics.
- **Mechanism:** a personalisation step over the existing cited data plus payment. Priced like a
  cheap report. Sold at the anxiety peak.
- **Build vs ops:** a genuine build (personalisation, payment, careful fence wording).
- **First steps (Phase 3 only):** define the brief template, the fence wording, and the payment flow;
  pilot on the proven corridor.
- **Success metric:** readers buy at a rate that beats the effort. **Kill criteria:** low conversion
  or the fence cannot be held without it reading as advice; then drop it.
- **Risk and fence:** the razor's edge is producing something personalised and useful that is still
  not advice. Legal review of the wording before launch. A flat PDF can feel cheap unless the cited
  rigor shows.

### Lane 4: the chosen one (money; exclusive deal; Phase 3)

- **What:** sell exclusive routing in one corridor to one firm for a retainer plus success fee,
  instead of many small affiliate links.
- **Mechanism:** scarcity is the product. The one firm gets all your high-intent readers in that
  corridor; you get predictable revenue and a reason to vet deeply; incentives align on quality.
- **Build vs ops:** pure deal-making, no build.
- **First steps (Phase 3 only):** take the corridor with the most proven traffic and the best lane-1
  track record, and convert that referral relationship into an exclusive retainer.
- **Success metric:** a signed retainer that beats the per-intro revenue it replaces. **Kill
  criteria:** no firm will pay a premium for exclusivity; stay on per-intro.
- **Risk:** exclusivity caps upside and couples the brand to one firm's behaviour; only safe once a
  corridor's volume is proven, and revisited if the firm slips.

### Lane 5: fix stale answers (traffic; ops; continuous from Phase 1)

- **What:** post genuinely useful, dated corrections where the current top answer is wrong.
- **Mechanism:** you uniquely know which facts changed, so generate a hit-list of the specific
  Reddit, Quora, and forum threads that rank for your corridors and whose top answer is now out of
  date, and post a real correction with the source and a sparing link back. Ninety percent genuine
  help.
- **Build vs ops:** ops, your hands, ongoing. A small helper to generate the hit-list from the
  changed-facts is optional later, not now.
- **First steps:** start this in Phase 1 on corridor one and never stop.
- **Success metric:** referral visits from those threads (visible in analytics). **Kill criteria:**
  threads consistently remove the posts; then shift weight to lane 6.
- **Risk:** forums police self-promotion hard; this only works as real help, and it does not scale.

### Lane 6: the reporter fact hook (traffic; ops; continuous from Phase 1)

- **What:** when a rule changes, hand journalists a ready-made, citable fact.
- **Mechanism:** ship a small "for press, for AI" page per change with the dated fact, the law
  number, and a one-line quote, and send it to the few people who cover this (IMI Daily,
  Schengen.News, the FT relocation desk, residency-by-investment trade press). One citation from a
  real outlet is a do-follow authority link and an AI-training signal at once.
- **Build vs ops:** mostly ops (outreach); the per-change page is a tiny content add.
- **First steps:** keep a short journalist list; fire on the next real rule change.
- **Success metric:** a citation from a real outlet. **Kill criteria:** sustained zero response; the
  effort is low, so this can idle between rule changes rather than be killed.
- **Risk:** low hit rate, needs a genuinely newsworthy change to peg to, but those happen often here.

## 6. The partner-vetting process (the bottleneck and the brand-risk control)

Lane 1 and lane 4 both route real people to professionals, so vetting is the constraint on how fast
you can add corridors and the single biggest brand risk. Minimum bar before routing anyone to a pro:
- a real, named, licensed practitioner in the corridor (not a lead-broker);
- evidence of competence (credentials, track record, a reference);
- a short trial: broker two or three intros and collect reader feedback before formalising;
- a feedback loop: every introduced reader is asked how it went; one bad report pauses the pro.

You add a corridor only when you can personally stand behind its professional. This is deliberately
slow and is the right kind of slow.

## 7. Measurement

The weekly digest is the dashboard. Per lane:
- Traffic (lanes 5, 6): referral visits by source (where AI and forum and press traffic show up),
  and indexing and queries from GSC once the key is in.
- Lane 1: intros requested, intros accepted, fees collected.
- Lane 2: paying practitioners, churn.
- Lane 3: brief sales and conversion rate.
- Lane 4: retainer value versus the per-intro revenue it replaced.

The leading indicator for the whole plan is corridor traffic plus intro-acceptance. If those two
move, the rest follows; if they do not, no later lane saves it.

## 8. Time and effort split (the honest shape)

- Founder hands, continuous: lanes 5 and 6, brokering lane 1, vetting, selling lanes 2 and 4.
- Real builds, small and few: email capture, the lane-1 intro block, later the lane-3 brief.
- Everything else is sales, outreach, and judgment, not code.

The weekly rhythm in Phase 1 is mostly posting corrections, watching for rule changes, and brokering
any intros by hand, with one short build (the intro block) up front.

## 9. Risks and the over-build guardrail

- **Traffic is the gate.** The money lanes do nothing without 5 and 6 working, so distribution is now
  and continuous, not later.
- **Vetting bottleneck and brand risk.** Covered in section 6; it caps lane-1 speed on purpose.
- **The over-build trap.** "Do all six" is a destination, not a build queue. The sequence is designed
  so you build three small things across the whole plan and let traffic and trust unlock the rest.
  Resist building the lane-1 platform, the lane-3 engine, or a content-generation tool before the
  concierge version has proven a pro will pay.
- **Thin at low volume.** Early traffic is small; the plan leans on value-per-visitor (high-ticket
  referral) precisely because volume will be low for a while.
- **Fence and legal.** Disclosure on paid relationships; legal review of lane-3 wording before
  launch; never a personal verdict.

## 10. The first executable slice

This is what becomes the first implementation plan and the first execution:

- **Build:** email capture on the corridor-one high-intent pages; the lane-1 "request a vetted
  introduction" block on corridor-one dealbreaker and qa pages (concierge: emails you plus the
  reader's screener inputs).
- **Ops, in parallel:** secure and vet one professional for corridor one; start lane 5 corrections on
  corridor one; keep the lane-6 journalist list ready.

Everything else (lanes 2, 3, 4, and widening 1) is explicitly out of scope for the first slice and
waits on its gate.
